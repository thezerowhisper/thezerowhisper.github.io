The Parkland Formula for Burn Resuscitation

A complete clinical guide to calculating fluid requirements in burn injuries — TBSA assessment, Rule of Nines, delivery timing, Modified Brooke, and common pitfalls.

Reviewed by an MBBS, AFIH Certified Physician  |  Based on ABA & WHO Burn Care Guidelines

Burns are among the most physiologically devastating injuries a human body can sustain. A large burn triggers a cascade of systemic responses — fluid pours out of blood vessels into surrounding tissues, blood pressure drops, organs fail. Without rapid and precisely calculated fluid resuscitation, a patient with a severe burn can die within hours from hypovolaemic shock — even if the burn itself does not seem immediately life-threatening.

The Parkland Formula is the most widely used calculation in burn resuscitation. It tells clinicians exactly how much intravenous fluid to give — and critically, when to give it — based on the patient's weight and the extent of the burn.

This guide covers everything you need to know: the formula itself, how to accurately estimate burn size, delivery timing, the Modified Brooke alternative, paediatric considerations, and common errors that can harm patients.

Why Do Burns Cause Such Severe Fluid Loss?

When the skin is burned, the barrier that normally keeps fluid inside the body is destroyed. Simultaneously, the burn triggers a massive inflammatory response: capillaries throughout the body — not just at the burn site — become "leaky," allowing plasma (the fluid component of blood) to pour into surrounding tissues.

This process, called capillary leak or burn oedema, is most intense in the first 8 hours after injury and continues for up to 48 hours. The result is a dramatic drop in circulating blood volume — called hypovolaemia — that can rapidly lead to shock, organ failure, and death if not corrected with IV fluids.

The goal of burn resuscitation is to replace this lost volume precisely — enough to maintain organ perfusion, but not so much that you cause dangerous fluid overload (a condition called oedema in unburned tissues, which can cause its own complications including abdominal compartment syndrome and pulmonary oedema).

The Parkland Formula

Developed by Dr Charles Baxter at Parkland Memorial Hospital in Dallas in the 1960s, the Parkland Formula estimates the total volume of Ringer's Lactate (RL) solution required in the first 24 hours after a burn injury.

Parkland Formula — First 24 Hours
Total Fluid (mL) = 4 mL × Body Weight (kg) × % TBSA Burned

TBSA = Total Body Surface Area burned (second and third degree burns only)
Fluid used: Ringer's Lactate (Hartmann's Solution)

This total volume is not given all at once. It is divided into two halves and administered over specific time windows:

First 8 Hours
½ of Total Volume
Counted from time of burn — not from arrival at hospital
Next 16 Hours
½ of Total Volume
Slower infusion rate over the remaining period

⚠️ Critical timing rule: The 8-hour clock starts from the time of the burn injury — not the time the patient arrives at hospital. If 3 hours have already passed by the time the patient reaches you, the first half must be delivered in the remaining 5 hours. This often requires a significantly higher initial infusion rate.

Worked Example

📋 Clinical Scenario

Patient: 40-year-old man, weight 70 kg, 35% TBSA burn (flame injury to trunk and both legs). Arrived at hospital 2 hours after injury.

Total fluid = 4 × 70 × 35 = 9,800 mL of Ringer's Lactate

First half (4,900 mL) to be given in the remaining 6 hours (8 hrs from burn − 2 hrs already elapsed) → Rate: 4,900 ÷ 6 = ~817 mL/hr

Second half (4,900 mL) over the next 16 hours → Rate: 4,900 ÷ 16 = ~306 mL/hr

These are starting rates. Adjust based on urine output (target 0.5–1 mL/kg/hr in adults) and clinical response — do not simply follow the calculated rate rigidly.

Estimating Burn Size — The Rule of Nines

The Parkland Formula is only as accurate as your TBSA estimate. Overestimating TBSA leads to fluid overload; underestimating leads to inadequate resuscitation. Only partial thickness (second degree) and full thickness (third degree) burns are counted — superficial (first degree) burns are excluded.

The most widely used method for adults is the Rule of Nines:

⚠️ The Rule of Nines is inaccurate in children. A child's head is proportionally much larger (up to 19% in an infant) and legs are smaller than in adults. For children, always use the Lund and Browder Chart, which accounts for age-specific proportions.

The Palmar Method — For Scattered or Patchy Burns

When burns are patchy, irregular, or scattered across the body, the Rule of Nines becomes difficult to apply. In these cases, use the Palmar Method: the patient's own palm (including fingers) represents approximately 1% of their TBSA. Count how many "palms" the burn covers to estimate percentage.

The Modified Brooke Formula — An Alternative

The Modified Brooke Formula is an alternative to Parkland, preferred by some centres because it uses a lower fluid volume (reducing the risk of fluid overload) and incorporates colloid in the second 24 hours.

Modified Brooke Formula — First 24 Hours
Total Fluid (mL) = 2 mL × Body Weight (kg) × % TBSA Burned

Fluid: Ringer's Lactate only in first 24 hours
Delivery: Same split — ½ in first 8 hrs, ½ in next 16 hrs

In the second 24 hours, Modified Brooke uses:

  • Colloid (albumin or FFP): 0.3–0.5 mL/kg/% TBSA to help restore oncotic pressure
  • Dextrose 5% in water (D5W): titrated to maintain urine output

Monitoring Resuscitation — Urine Output Is Key

The Parkland formula gives you a starting point — not a rigid prescription. The primary endpoint for titrating fluid resuscitation is urine output, measured via a urinary catheter:

PatientTarget Urine Output
Adults0.5 mL/kg/hr (approximately 30–50 mL/hr)
Children (< 30 kg)1 mL/kg/hr
Electrical burns / myoglobinuria1–1.5 mL/kg/hr (to flush myoglobin and prevent renal tubular damage)
  • If urine output is too low → increase the infusion rate by 20%
  • If urine output is too high → reduce the infusion rate by 20%
  • Never use diuretics to increase urine output in burn resuscitation — this masks inadequate volume replacement

⚠️ "Fluid Creep" — giving significantly more fluid than the Parkland formula predicts — is a recognised and dangerous phenomenon in burn care. It is associated with abdominal compartment syndrome, pulmonary oedema, and limb compartment syndrome. Avoid aggressive over-resuscitation. Aim for the lower end of the urine output target range.

Paediatric Considerations

Children are not simply small adults when it comes to burn resuscitation. Key differences:

  • TBSA estimation: Always use the Lund and Browder Chart — the Rule of Nines is inaccurate in children under 15 years
  • Fluid formula: Same Parkland calculation (3–4 mL/kg/%TBSA), but add maintenance fluids on top (calculated by the Holliday-Segar method) — children have higher baseline fluid requirements
  • Glucose monitoring: Children, especially infants, are at risk of hypoglycaemia during resuscitation — monitor blood glucose regularly and use glucose-containing maintenance fluids
  • Urine output target: 1 mL/kg/hr (higher than adults)
  • Hypothermia risk: Children lose heat rapidly through burned skin — keep the environment warm and use warm fluids

When to Transfer to a Specialist Burns Centre

Not all burns can be managed at a general hospital. Transfer to a specialist burns centre is indicated for:

  • Burns > 10% TBSA in children or elderly; > 20% TBSA in adults
  • Any full thickness (third degree) burn
  • Burns involving face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, or major joints
  • Circumferential burns of limbs or chest
  • Inhalation injury (suspected or confirmed)
  • Electrical or chemical burns
  • Burns in patients with significant comorbidities (diabetes, immunosuppression, cardiac disease)
  • Burns in pregnant women

A Note on Inhalation Injury

Inhalation injury — caused by breathing hot gases, steam, or toxic combustion products — significantly increases fluid requirements beyond what the Parkland formula predicts. Patients with confirmed or suspected inhalation injury may require 30–50% more fluid than calculated.

Suspect inhalation injury if the patient was in an enclosed space, has singed nasal hairs or eyebrows, carbonaceous (sooty) sputum, a hoarse voice, stridor, or facial burns. These patients should be intubated early — airway oedema can develop rapidly and make late intubation extremely difficult or impossible.

Key Takeaways

  • Total fluid (mL) = 4 × weight (kg) × % TBSA — using Ringer's Lactate only in first 24 hours
  • Give ½ in first 8 hours (from time of burn), ½ in next 16 hours
  • Count only partial and full thickness burns in TBSA — exclude superficial (first degree)
  • Use Rule of Nines for adults; use Lund and Browder Chart for children
  • Target urine output: 0.5 mL/kg/hr in adults, 1 mL/kg/hr in children
  • Adjust rates hourly based on urine output — do not rigidly follow the calculated rate
  • Avoid fluid creep — over-resuscitation causes serious complications
  • Add maintenance fluids in children; increase volume targets in inhalation injury
