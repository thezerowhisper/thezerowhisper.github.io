Conception Date Calculator: How to Find Out When You Conceived

Calculate your exact conception date from your due date, last menstrual period, IVF transfer date, or ultrasound — plus ovulation windows, IVF dating, and week-by-week fetal development.

Reviewed by an MBBS, AFIH Certified Physician  |  Based on FOGSI, ACOG & WHO Obstetric Dating Guidelines

One of the most common questions in early pregnancy — asked in doctor's offices, whispered in family groups, and typed into search engines millions of times every month — is simply: when did I conceive?

It is a question that carries significant emotional weight. For couples who planned their pregnancy, it pins down a moment of hopeful possibility. For those surprised by a positive test, it helps establish a timeline. For women who have been through IVF, it can be calculated with remarkable precision. And for anyone preparing for their first antenatal scan, it forms the foundation of everything that follows — gestational age, due date, trimester milestones, and the entire schedule of scans and tests that make up antenatal care.

This guide explains exactly how conception date is calculated — from every starting point — and what the result means for your pregnancy.

What Is the Conception Date — and Is It Different from the Due Date?

Conception is the moment a sperm successfully fertilises an egg, creating a zygote — the single cell from which every cell in a human body will ultimately develop. This event, called fertilisation, typically occurs in the fallopian tube within 12–24 hours of ovulation.

The conception date and the due date (EDD) are fundamentally different things — but they are mathematically linked. The EDD is calculated as 280 days (40 weeks) from the first day of the last menstrual period (LMP). But conception does not occur on the first day of the period — it occurs around 14 days later, at ovulation, in a standard 28-day cycle.

So the relationship is:

Core Relationship
EDD = LMP + 280 days (40 weeks)
Conception Date = LMP + ~14 days
Conception Date = EDD − 266 days (38 weeks)

A pregnancy is conventionally counted as 40 weeks from LMP — but is only approximately 38 weeks from actual conception. Both ways of counting are used in different contexts.

This is why obstetricians measure gestational age from LMP (not conception) — most women know when their last period started, but very few know exactly when they ovulated or conceived.

Method 1: Calculating Conception Date from Your Due Date

If your doctor has already told you your estimated due date — whether based on LMP or confirmed by ultrasound — you can work backwards to find your approximate conception date.

From Due Date (EDD)
Conception Date = Due Date − 266 days

Example: EDD = 15 January 2026 → Conception Date = 23 April 2025
(15 Jan − 266 days = ~23 April of the previous year)

This is the simplest and quickest calculation. It is particularly useful when you already have a confirmed ultrasound-based EDD, which is more accurate than one calculated purely from LMP.

Method 2: Calculating Conception Date from Your Last Menstrual Period (LMP)

For a standard 28-day cycle, ovulation — and therefore the conception window — occurs around Day 14 of the cycle, counting from Day 1 (the first day of the period). Conception is then:

From LMP (Standard 28-day cycle)
Conception Date ≈ LMP + 14 days

For cycles longer than 28 days, add the extra days. For a 35-day cycle: LMP + 21 days.
Formula: Conception Date = LMP + (Cycle Length − 14) days

Adjusting for Cycle Length

Not everyone has a 28-day cycle. In fact, among Indian women, cycle length can range widely from 21 to 45 days — and the ovulation window shifts accordingly. The key insight is that the luteal phase (post-ovulation) is almost always 14 days in a normal cycle. It is the follicular phase (pre-ovulation) that varies.

The Conception Window — When Can You Actually Get Pregnant?

Many people believe that conception can only happen on the single day of ovulation. In reality, the fertile window spans 5–7 days around ovulation, because sperm can survive in the female reproductive tract for up to 5 days, while the egg survives for only 12–24 hours after ovulation.

This fertile window is the reason the conception date is always an estimate — if intercourse occurred on Day 10 but ovulation happened on Day 14, the sperm survived four days in the tract before fertilising the egg. The "conception date" in this scenario is technically Day 14, not Day 10.

Method 3: Conception Date from IVF or Frozen Embryo Transfer (FET)

IVF is the one scenario where the conception date can be calculated with near-perfect precision — because fertilisation happened in a laboratory at a known time, and the exact age of the embryo at transfer is documented.

Day 2 Transfer
EDD = Transfer + 264 days

Conception Date = Transfer Date − 2 days (the day of egg retrieval and fertilisation)

Day 3 Transfer
EDD = Transfer + 263 days

Conception Date = Transfer Date − 3 days

Day 5 Blastocyst (most common)
EDD = Transfer + 261 days

Conception Date = Transfer Date − 5 days. Most FET cycles use Day 5 blastocysts.

Day 6 Extended Blastocyst
EDD = Transfer + 260 days

Conception Date = Transfer Date − 6 days. Used for embryos that took longer to develop.

✅ Master formula for IVF: EDD = Transfer Date + (266 − Embryo Age in days). This is verified against ACOG and FOGSI IVF dating guidelines and is the standard used by IVF centres worldwide.

For frozen embryo transfer (FET) cycles, the same formula applies — the embryo's age at the time of freezing is factored into the calculation. Your IVF clinic should document the embryo's precise developmental stage in your records.

Method 4: Conception Date from Ultrasound (Crown-Rump Length)

An early ultrasound performed between 7 and 13 weeks gestation measures the crown-rump length (CRL) — the length of the embryo/fetus from the top of the head to the base of the spine. CRL is the most accurate method of dating a pregnancy and can place the gestational age within ±3–5 days.

From the gestational age given by ultrasound, the conception date is calculated as:

From Ultrasound CRL
Conception Date = Date of Scan − Gestational Age (in days) + 14 days

Example: Scan on 10 June, gestational age 9 weeks 0 days (63 days):
Conception Date = 10 June − 63 days + 14 days = 10 June − 49 days = 22 April

The 14-day addition accounts for the fact that gestational age is counted from LMP (Day 0), while conception occurred approximately 14 days later. An ultrasound reporting 9 weeks means 9 weeks since LMP — so conception was approximately 7 weeks (9 − 2 weeks) before the scan.

🔊 When your dates change after a scan: If the ultrasound EDD differs from your LMP-based EDD by more than 7 days in the first trimester, the ultrasound date is used as the official EDD. This is standard FOGSI and ACOG practice.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I find my exact conception date?
For most natural pregnancies, conception date is an estimate — not an exact date. Since sperm can survive 3–5 days in the reproductive tract, the egg could have been fertilised several days after intercourse occurred. IVF pregnancies are the exception — because fertilisation happened in a laboratory at a documented time, the conception date is known precisely to within hours.
My period is irregular — how do I calculate my conception date?
LMP-based calculation is unreliable for irregular cycles. The most accurate method is an early ultrasound (7–13 weeks) measuring crown-rump length (CRL). If you have an ultrasound report with a gestational age, use that as your starting point. Women with PCOS, thyroid disorders, or very short or long cycles should always rely on ultrasound dating.
Is the conception date the same as the date of intercourse?
Not necessarily. Sperm can survive in the fallopian tube for up to 5 days. If you had intercourse on Monday and ovulated on Thursday, the egg was fertilised on Thursday — the conception date is Thursday, not Monday. This is why conception dates are estimates, not certainties, in naturally conceived pregnancies.
My ultrasound date is different from my LMP date — which is correct?
If the difference is more than 7 days in the first trimester, the ultrasound date is used as the official due date. This is the standard followed by FOGSI and ACOG. Early ultrasound (7–13 weeks) using CRL is the most accurate dating method and takes precedence over LMP. After 20 weeks, ultrasound dating is less reliable and the first trimester date is usually kept.
How does IVF conception date work differently?
In IVF, the egg is retrieved and fertilised in a laboratory on a known date. The embryo is then cultured for 2–6 days before being transferred to the uterus. The conception date is the egg retrieval/fertilisation date — not the transfer date. For a Day 5 blastocyst transfer, the conception date is 5 days before the transfer date. Your IVF clinic will have documented the embryo's exact developmental stage.
Why does gestational age start from LMP, not from conception?
Historically, before modern obstetric dating, LMP was the only reliable date most women could recall. Ovulation and conception are invisible events — there was no way to know exactly when they occurred. The LMP-based convention, established by Franz Naegele in the 19th century, standardised dating around a known, observable date. It adds approximately 2 weeks to the "true" age of the pregnancy from conception, which is why a "40-week" pregnancy is actually only about 38 weeks from fertilisation.

Early Signs of Pregnancy After Conception

After fertilisation, the embryo travels down the fallopian tube and implants in the uterine wall approximately 6–12 days after conception. At this point, the embryo begins producing human chorionic gonadotrophin (hCG) — the hormone detected by pregnancy tests. Symptoms, if they appear, typically begin around 4–6 weeks gestational age (2–4 weeks after conception):

  • Missed period — the most reliable early sign
  • Breast tenderness and swelling — often the first symptom, caused by rising progesterone
  • Nausea (morning sickness) — usually begins around 6 weeks gestational age; peaks at 8–10 weeks
  • Fatigue — progesterone causes marked tiredness in early pregnancy
  • Implantation bleeding — light spotting 6–12 days after conception; often mistaken for a light period
  • Frequent urination — increasing hCG and growing uterus put pressure on the bladder
  • Heightened sense of smell — a characteristic early pregnancy symptom caused by oestrogen

✅ When to take a pregnancy test: Home urine pregnancy tests detect hCG reliably from the first day of a missed period — approximately 14 days after conception. Testing earlier may give a false negative even if conception has occurred, because hCG levels are not yet high enough to detect. For the most accurate result, test with the first morning urine (most concentrated).

Conception Timing in Indian Women — What's Different

Several factors make conception timing calculations particularly important to individualise for Indian women:

  • PCOS prevalence: PCOS affects approximately 20% of Indian women and causes highly irregular cycles with unpredictable ovulation. LMP-based conception dating is often significantly inaccurate — early ultrasound dating is essential.
  • Thyroid disease: India has very high rates of hypothyroidism (particularly in women) — thyroid dysfunction causes irregular cycles and may affect both ovulation timing and early pregnancy outcomes. Check TSH early in pregnancy.
  • Nutritional anaemia: Iron deficiency and nutritional anaemia — extremely common in Indian women — can cause irregular cycles and affect the reliability of LMP-based dating.
  • Rising IVF rates: India performs over 250,000 IVF cycles annually, making IVF conception date calculation increasingly relevant for a large proportion of pregnancies.
  • Gestational diabetes (GDM): Indian women have 4× the global average risk of GDM — early and accurate pregnancy dating is critical for ensuring GDM screening (75g OGTT) is done at the correct gestational age (24–28 weeks).

Key Takeaways

  • Conception date ≈ Due date − 266 days, or LMP + 14 days (for a 28-day cycle)
  • For irregular cycles or PCOS, LMP-based calculation is unreliable — use early ultrasound (CRL at 7–13 weeks)
  • The fertile window spans 5–7 days — sperm survives up to 5 days; the egg lives 12–24 hours after ovulation
  • IVF conception date = Transfer date minus embryo age. Day 5 blastocyst: EDD = Transfer + 261 days
  • Gestational age is always counted from LMP, not conception — this adds ~14 days to the count
  • If ultrasound EDD differs from LMP EDD by more than 7 days, the scan date takes precedence
  • In India: PCOS, thyroid disease, and anaemia make ultrasound dating especially important for accurate conception timing
  • Pregnancy tests become reliable 14 days after conception — the day of a missed period
