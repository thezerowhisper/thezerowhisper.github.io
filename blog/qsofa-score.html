qSOFA Score: Rapid Sepsis Screening Outside the ICU

The 3 qSOFA criteria explained, what score ≥ 2 means, how it relates to full SOFA and Sepsis-3, its accuracy vs SIRS, when to escalate, and using qSOFA in Indian ward settings.

Reviewed by an MBBS, AFIH Certified Physician  |  Based on Sepsis-3 Consensus (2016) & ISCCM Guidelines

Sepsis kills. It kills quickly. And it often starts — in an emergency department, on a general medical ward, in a rural clinic — without the full battery of laboratory tests that are needed to calculate the SOFA score. A patient with pneumonia, a urinary tract infection, or an infected wound can go from "unwell" to critically ill in hours, and the window for effective intervention is narrow.

The quick SOFA (qSOFA) was designed for exactly this situation. Three clinical observations — respiratory rate, blood pressure, and mental status — that can be assessed at the bedside in under 60 seconds, without any blood tests, without any laboratory equipment. A score of ≥ 2 in a patient with suspected infection is a call to action: assess urgently, check lactate, send cultures, and start antibiotics.

The Three qSOFA Criteria

Each criterion scores 1 point. Maximum score: 3.

RR
1 point

Respiratory Rate ≥ 22/min

Tachypnoea at rest reflects respiratory compensation or pulmonary involvement in sepsis. Count for a full 60 seconds — do not estimate.

BP
1 point

Systolic BP ≤ 100 mmHg

Hypotension reflects haemodynamic compromise. Any single reading of SBP ≤ 100 scores — does not need to be sustained.

MS
1 point

Altered Mental Status

GCS < 15 — any new confusion, disorientation, or reduced consciousness. This is the most specific criterion for sepsis-associated encephalopathy.

Score Interpretation and Action

0 – 1
LOW RISK
Sepsis less likely. Continue to monitor. Reassess if clinical condition changes. Treat the underlying infection if present.
≥ 2
HIGH RISK — ACT NOW
Suspected sepsis. Immediate senior review. Measure lactate. Blood cultures × 2. IV antibiotics within 1 hour. IV access and fluid assessment.

⚠️ qSOFA < 2 does NOT rule out sepsis. qSOFA has low sensitivity (~50–60%). A patient scoring 0–1 with clinical signs of severe infection still requires full assessment. Use qSOFA to escalate — not to reassure.

qSOFA vs SIRS — Why the Change Was Made

Before Sepsis-3, sepsis was identified using the SIRS (Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome) criteria — temperature > 38°C or < 36°C, HR > 90, RR > 20, WBC < 4 or > 12 × 10³/µL. Sepsis was defined as SIRS + suspected infection.

The problem: SIRS criteria are too sensitive and too non-specific. A patient post-exercise, a woman in early labour, a patient with a simple viral URTI — all can meet SIRS criteria. Over 90% of ICU patients at any given time meet SIRS, making it meaningless as a sepsis discriminator.

The Hour-1 Sepsis Bundle — What to Do When qSOFA ≥ 2

A qSOFA ≥ 2 in a patient with suspected infection should trigger the Hour-1 Sepsis Bundle (Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2018):

  1. Measure serum lactate. If lactate > 2 mmol/L, remeasure after resuscitation. Lactate > 4 = tissue hypoperfusion, higher mortality.
  2. Blood cultures × 2 before antibiotics. If delay is anticipated, do not withhold antibiotics for cultures in a deteriorating patient.
  3. IV broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour of sepsis recognition. Time to antibiotics is the most important modifiable outcome predictor.
  4. 30 mL/kg IV crystalloid if hypotensive (SBP < 90 or MAP < 65) or lactate ≥ 4 mmol/L. Reassess after each bolus.
  5. Vasopressors if hypotension persists — target MAP ≥ 65 mmHg. Norepinephrine is first-line.

qSOFA in Indian Clinical Settings

qSOFA is particularly valuable in India's clinical context for several reasons:

  • Resource-limited settings: Many district hospitals, PHCs, and rural facilities do not have immediate access to full blood count, serum lactate, or ABG. qSOFA can identify high-risk patients with only a BP cuff, a watch, and a clinical assessment.
  • Tropical infection burden: Sepsis from dengue, malaria, leptospira, scrub typhus, and enteric fever — common in Indian wards — can deteriorate rapidly. qSOFA ≥ 2 in a febrile patient with any of these suspected diagnoses should lower the threshold for ICU referral.
  • Nurse-led triage: In many Indian hospitals, the first clinical contact is a nursing assessment. Training nurses to calculate qSOFA as part of triage vitals can significantly reduce time to physician review for high-risk patients.
  • Emergency departments: Indian emergency departments are frequently overwhelmed. A simple 3-parameter qSOFA score calculated at triage helps prioritise which patients need immediate attention versus those who can safely wait.

✅ Practical implementation tip: Print a qSOFA calculation chart at every nursing station and triage point. Any patient with suspected infection and qSOFA ≥ 2 should automatically generate a "sepsis alert" to the treating physician — regardless of whether the BP is "just" 100 mmHg or the RR is "only" 22.

Limitations of qSOFA

  • Low sensitivity (~50–60%): Half of sepsis patients with organ dysfunction may not score ≥ 2 on qSOFA. A low score must not be used to rule out sepsis — clinical judgement always takes precedence.
  • Not validated for ICU patients: In the ICU, full SOFA is the appropriate tool. qSOFA was specifically validated for non-ICU settings.
  • Mental status assessment is subjective: "New confusion" requires knowing the patient's baseline. In elderly patients with dementia, or after sedative medication, interpretation is difficult.
  • Does not capture all organ dysfunction: Renal failure, hepatic dysfunction, and coagulopathy — major components of sepsis organ failure — are not captured by qSOFA.
  • Not designed for children: Paediatric vital sign norms differ from adults. Use PEWS (Paediatric Early Warning Score) or validated paediatric sepsis tools for children.

Key Takeaways

  • qSOFA uses 3 criteria: RR ≥ 22, SBP ≤ 100, Altered mental status — all assessable in 60 seconds, no labs needed
  • qSOFA ≥ 2 in a patient with suspected infection = probable sepsis → escalate immediately
  • qSOFA < 2 does NOT rule out sepsis — sensitivity is only ~50–60%
  • qSOFA is for outside-ICU screening; full SOFA is for ICU diagnosis and prognostication
  • qSOFA ≥ 2 triggers the Hour-1 bundle: lactate + cultures + antibiotics + fluids ± vasopressors
  • qSOFA replaced SIRS criteria — SIRS was too non-specific (~10% specificity) to be meaningful
  • In India: particularly valuable for resource-limited settings, nurse-led triage, and tropical infections
  • qSOFA is not validated for children — use paediatric-specific tools
