BMI for Indians: Why India Uses Different Cutoffs & What Your Number Means

Why the global BMI scale does not fully apply to South Asians, what India-specific BMI cutoffs are, the real limitations of BMI, and how to interpret your metabolic risk more accurately.

Reviewed by an MBBS, AFIH Certified Physician  |  Based on WHO Asian Cutoffs & ICMR Guidelines

BMI — Body Mass Index — is one of the most widely used numbers in medicine. It appears on every health report, drives insurance premiums, and determines eligibility for certain surgical procedures. Most people have seen their BMI at some point and wondered what it means.

But here is something many people in India do not know: the standard BMI cutoffs used globally — developed primarily from data on European populations — do not accurately reflect metabolic risk in South Asians. An Indian person classified as "normal weight" by global standards may already have a significantly elevated risk of type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease. Indian health authorities, including the ICMR, use lower, Asia-specific BMI thresholds for this reason.

This guide explains what BMI is, how it is calculated, why India uses different cutoffs, what your number actually means for your health, and — critically — what BMI cannot tell you.

What Is BMI and How Is It Calculated?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a simple ratio of weight to height squared. It was developed by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 19th century as a population-level statistical measure — not originally as a clinical tool for individuals.

BMI Formula
BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ Height² (m²)

Example: A person weighing 70 kg and 1.70 m tall has a BMI of 70 ÷ (1.70 × 1.70) = 70 ÷ 2.89 = 24.2 kg/m²

BMI is a dimensionless number expressed in kg/m². It requires only a scale and a measuring tape — making it the most practical population-level screening tool for weight-related health risk available.

Global BMI Categories vs. Indian (Asian) BMI Categories

The standard WHO global BMI categories were derived largely from studies of European populations. In 2004, the WHO and multiple Asian health organisations — including experts from India — recommended lower action points for Asian populations, recognising that South Asians develop metabolic risk at lower BMI values than their European counterparts.

The key difference: the "at risk" category begins at BMI 23 for Indians — not 25 as in global standards. This means a person with a BMI of 23.5 would be classified as "normal" by international standards but "overweight / at risk" by Indian criteria, and would be advised to make lifestyle changes.

Why Do Indians Develop Risk at Lower BMI?

This is one of the most important and clinically validated findings in South Asian metabolic research. There are several well-documented reasons:

Higher Body Fat Percentage at the Same BMI

At an equivalent BMI, South Asian individuals tend to have a higher percentage of body fat than Europeans. This is sometimes called the "thin-fat Indian" phenotype — individuals who appear lean by BMI but have elevated visceral and subcutaneous fat, particularly around the abdomen.

Greater Visceral Adiposity

South Asians store more fat viscerally — around the internal organs (liver, pancreas) — compared to subcutaneous fat (under the skin). Visceral fat is metabolically far more harmful than subcutaneous fat. It releases inflammatory cytokines, disrupts insulin signalling, and directly drives insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular disease.

Lower Muscle Mass

On average, South Asians have lower skeletal muscle mass for a given body weight compared to European populations. Since BMI does not distinguish between muscle and fat, the same BMI in a South Asian individual represents relatively more fat and less muscle.

Genetic Predisposition to Insulin Resistance

South Asians have a significantly higher genetic predisposition to insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes, independent of obesity. Indian populations develop T2DM at younger ages and lower BMI values than European populations, and the disease progresses more aggressively.

Waist Circumference — The Measurement BMI Misses

Because BMI cannot distinguish fat distribution, waist circumference is an important complementary measure — it directly reflects visceral adiposity.

Conditions Associated with High BMI and Abdominal Obesity in Indians

Type 2 Diabetes

Indians develop T2DM at younger ages and lower BMI than global averages. Every 1 kg/m² rise in BMI increases T2DM risk by ~9%.

Cardiovascular Disease

Visceral obesity drives dyslipidaemia, hypertension, and atherosclerosis. Indians have disproportionately high rates of early-onset CAD.

Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD)

Very common in Indians at BMI as low as 23–25. Now renamed MASLD (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease).

Hypertension

Visceral fat increases sodium retention and activates the renin-angiotensin system, driving blood pressure elevation.

Obstructive Sleep Apnoea

Even modest central obesity significantly increases OSA risk in South Asians — often underdiagnosed.

Metabolic Syndrome

A cluster of abdominal obesity + insulin resistance + dyslipidaemia + hypertension. Extremely common in urban Indians.

What BMI Cannot Tell You — Its Limitations

BMI is a useful screening tool — but it has important limitations that every person should understand:

  • It does not distinguish fat from muscle. A bodybuilder with very high muscle mass will have a high BMI but minimal metabolic risk. An elderly person with low muscle mass (sarcopenia) may have a "normal" BMI but high body fat percentage and significant risk.
  • It does not indicate fat distribution. A person with all their fat around their abdomen (visceral, high-risk) and a person with the same BMI but fat around their hips and thighs (subcutaneous, lower-risk) are classified identically — yet have very different risk profiles.
  • It does not account for age, sex, or ethnicity adequately. Older adults naturally lose muscle and gain fat — BMI does not reflect this. Women naturally have higher body fat than men at the same BMI.
  • It was not designed for clinical use in individuals. Quetelet developed BMI as a population-statistics tool. Using it to make individual clinical decisions requires additional context.

⚠️ BMI is a starting point, not a complete picture. A normal BMI does not guarantee metabolic health. A high BMI does not automatically mean poor health. Always interpret BMI alongside waist circumference, fasting blood glucose, lipid profile, blood pressure, and family history.

Beyond BMI — A More Complete Metabolic Assessment

For Indians, particularly those with a family history of diabetes or heart disease, a full metabolic screening includes:

  • Waist circumference (measured at the midpoint between the lowest rib and the iliac crest, after a gentle exhale)
  • Fasting blood glucose or HbA1c — to detect prediabetes or undiagnosed T2DM
  • Lipid profile — triglycerides, LDL, HDL cholesterol
  • Blood pressure measurement
  • Liver function tests and ultrasound if NAFLD is suspected (abdominal obesity + fatigue)
  • Thyroid function (TSH) — hypothyroidism can cause weight gain and is common in Indian women

This combination — rather than BMI alone — gives a far more accurate picture of metabolic risk, and is particularly important for Indians where risk emerges at lower BMI values than global standards assume.

What Can You Do About a High BMI?

The good news: metabolic risk associated with excess weight is substantially reversible with lifestyle changes. Even a 5–10% reduction in body weight produces clinically meaningful reductions in blood sugar, blood pressure, triglycerides, and cardiovascular risk.

  • Diet: Reduce refined carbohydrates (white rice, maida, sugar), increase dietary fibre (dal, vegetables, whole grains), adequate protein (60–80 g/day). A moderate caloric deficit of 300–500 kcal/day is sustainable and safe.
  • Exercise: At least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise per week (brisk walking, cycling, swimming) plus 2 sessions of resistance training. Even 30 minutes of walking per day produces measurable metabolic benefits.
  • Sleep: Poor sleep is a major and underappreciated driver of insulin resistance and weight gain. Target 7–8 hours of quality sleep.
  • Stress management: Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which promotes visceral fat accumulation. Yoga, meditation, and regular physical activity all help.
  • Medical evaluation: If BMI is ≥ 27.5 with comorbidities (diabetes, hypertension, NAFLD) or ≥ 32.5 without, medical or surgical weight management may be discussed with your doctor.

Key Takeaways

  • BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ Height² (m²) — a simple screening measure, not a complete health assessment
  • Indian/Asian BMI cutoffs are lower — "at risk" starts at BMI 23, not 25 as in global standards
  • South Asians develop higher body fat and visceral adiposity at lower BMI values than Europeans
  • Waist circumference (men >90 cm, women >80 cm in Indians) is a critical complementary measure BMI misses
  • BMI cannot distinguish fat from muscle and does not indicate fat distribution
  • A "normal" BMI does not guarantee metabolic health — especially in Indians with abdominal obesity
  • Even a 5–10% weight reduction produces clinically meaningful improvements in metabolic risk factors
