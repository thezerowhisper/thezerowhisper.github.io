SOFA Score: Organ Failure, Sepsis-3 & ICU Mortality

How to calculate the SOFA score across all 6 organ systems, what each score predicts for ICU mortality, the Sepsis-3 definition, delta-SOFA, qSOFA, and how to use SOFA in practice.

Reviewed by an MBBS, AFIH Certified Physician  |  Based on Sepsis-3 Consensus (2016) & ESICM Guidelines

Sepsis is among the most dangerous conditions treated in hospitals worldwide — and also one of the most misunderstood. It is not simply an infection. It is the body's dysregulated response to infection, causing organ dysfunction that can rapidly spiral into multi-organ failure and death. Globally, sepsis causes an estimated 11 million deaths per year — more than most cancers.

The SOFA Score (Sequential Organ Failure Assessment) is the cornerstone tool for assessing the degree of organ dysfunction in critically ill patients, defining sepsis according to the Sepsis-3 consensus criteria, predicting ICU mortality, and tracking whether a patient is improving or deteriorating over time.

Understanding the SOFA score is essential for any clinician working in emergency medicine, internal medicine, or critical care.

What Is the SOFA Score?

The SOFA score was developed in 1994 (originally as the Sepsis-related Organ Failure Assessment) to objectively quantify the degree of organ dysfunction in ICU patients. It evaluates six organ systems, scoring each from 0 (normal) to 4 (most severely impaired), giving a maximum total of 24.

These mortality estimates are population-level associations from ICU studies — they are not individual prognoses. A patient with SOFA 14 may survive with excellent care; a patient with SOFA 6 may deteriorate rapidly if the underlying cause is not controlled. Use SOFA as one input into clinical decision-making, not as a single determinant of prognosis or treatment intensity.

The Sepsis-3 Definition — Why SOFA Matters

In 2016, the Third International Consensus Definitions Task Force (Sepsis-3) published a landmark update to the definitions of sepsis and septic shock. SOFA is central to the new definition.

Sepsis-3 Consensus Definitions (2016)
Sepsis: Life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection
→ Operationally: suspected infection + acute SOFA increase ≥ 2 points from baseline

Septic Shock: Sepsis + vasopressor requirement to maintain MAP ≥ 65 mmHg + serum lactate > 2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation
→ Hospital mortality of septic shock exceeds 40%

The key change from older definitions: the word "infection" alone is no longer sufficient to diagnose sepsis. There must be demonstrable organ dysfunction — operationally defined as an acute SOFA increase of ≥ 2. This replaced the older SIRS-based definition (which was too sensitive and non-specific — simple fever and tachycardia met criteria for "sepsis").

Delta-SOFA — The Most Clinically Important Metric

A single SOFA score at admission is useful but limited. The change in SOFA score (delta-SOFA) over 24–48 hours is far more predictive of outcome than any single reading:

  • Rising SOFA (delta-SOFA ≥ +2 within 48 hours) → significant increase in mortality; consider escalation, family discussions, palliative planning if appropriate
  • Stable SOFA → patient is holding but not improving; monitor closely and reassess treatment
  • Falling SOFA → patient is improving; treatment is working; consider de-escalation when appropriate

✅ Best practice: Score SOFA on ICU admission and then every 24–48 hours. Document the trend in the patient's notes. A worsening SOFA trajectory in the first 48 hours is one of the strongest predictors of ICU mortality available at the bedside.

qSOFA — The Bedside Screening Tool

The full SOFA score requires laboratory results and is designed for ICU patients. For screening patients outside the ICU (in the ED, ward, or outpatient setting) who may be developing sepsis, the qSOFA (quick SOFA) provides a rapid 3-point bedside assessment:

qSOFA CriterionPoints
Altered mental status (GCS < 15)1
Respiratory rate ≥ 22 breaths/min1
Systolic blood pressure ≤ 100 mmHg1

A qSOFA score ≥ 2 in a patient with suspected infection should prompt urgent clinical assessment, lactate measurement, blood cultures, and consideration of ICU-level care. qSOFA requires no laboratory tests — it can be calculated in seconds at the bedside.

⚠️ qSOFA has low sensitivity — a score < 2 does not rule out sepsis. It is a screening prompt, not a diagnostic tool. SOFA ≥ 2 from baseline (with laboratory values) is the diagnostic criterion for sepsis.

Sepsis Management — The "Hour-1 Bundle"

Surviving Sepsis Campaign guidelines recommend completing the following bundle within the first hour of sepsis recognition:

  1. Measure lactate. If lactate > 2 mmol/L, remeasure after fluid resuscitation. Lactate > 4 mmol/L indicates tissue hypoperfusion — a marker of septic shock even if BP is preserved.
  2. Blood cultures × 2 before starting antibiotics (do not delay antibiotics for cultures if patient is deteriorating).
  3. Broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour of sepsis recognition. Time to antibiotics is the single most important modifiable predictor of sepsis mortality.
  4. 30 mL/kg IV crystalloid bolus for hypotension or lactate ≥ 4 mmol/L. Reassess fluid responsiveness before further boluses.
  5. Vasopressors if hypotension persists despite fluids — target MAP ≥ 65 mmHg. Norepinephrine is first-line vasopressor.

SOFA in the Indian ICU Context

India has a significant and growing ICU burden. Several important considerations apply to SOFA use in Indian critical care settings:

  • Tropical infections dominate: Dengue (thrombocytopenia), malaria (multi-organ failure), leptospirosis (liver and kidney), scrub typhus, and enteric fever are common causes of high SOFA scores in Indian ICUs — distinct from the pneumonia/urosepsis predominance in Western series
  • ABG availability: PaO₂/FiO₂ requires arterial blood gas — not always available in all settings. SpO₂/FiO₂ ratio is a validated surrogate when ABG is unavailable
  • Vasopressor choice: Norepinephrine is first-line; dopamine is still used in many Indian centres but associated with higher mortality in septic shock in RCT data
  • Early antibiotics are critical: De-escalation once cultures return should be actively practised to reduce antimicrobial resistance, which is a major and growing problem in Indian hospitals

Key Takeaways

  • SOFA scores 6 organ systems (Respiratory, Coagulation, Liver, Cardiovascular, CNS, Renal) — maximum 24
  • Sepsis-3 definition: Suspected infection + acute SOFA increase ≥ 2 from baseline
  • Septic shock = sepsis + vasopressors + lactate > 2 despite fluids → mortality > 40%
  • Delta-SOFA trend (24–48h change) is more predictive than a single score — score serially
  • qSOFA ≥ 2 (mental status + RR + SBP) = prompt urgent sepsis assessment — no labs needed
  • Hour-1 bundle: lactate, cultures, antibiotics within 1 hour, IV fluids, vasopressors if needed
  • In India: consider tropical infections (dengue, malaria, leptospira) as major causes of high SOFA
  • SOFA mortality estimates are population-level — not individual prognoses; use with clinical context
