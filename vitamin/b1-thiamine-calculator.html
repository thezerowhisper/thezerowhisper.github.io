Wernicke–Korsakoff Syndrome

Wernicke encephalopathy presents with the classic triad of confusion, ophthalmoplegia, and ataxia — but the full triad occurs in only ~16% of cases. Always treat empirically with high-dose IV thiamine in at-risk patients (alcoholics, malnourished, post-bariatric surgery, prolonged vomiting). Untreated Wernicke's leads to Korsakoff psychosis (irreversible in 80%). High-dose IV thiamine 500 mg TDS × 3 days is the SIGN/EFNS standard.

Beriberi — Still Relevant in India

Beriberi remains clinically relevant in India — particularly in rice-eating populations (eastern/northeastern states), prison/institutional settings, chronic alcohol users, and patients on prolonged IV fluids without B-vitamins. Wet beriberi (high-output cardiac failure) is life-threatening but responds dramatically to IV thiamine within 24–48 hours. Dry beriberi (peripheral neuropathy) recovers more slowly with oral thiamine.

Critical Warning — Thiamine and Glucose

Administering IV dextrose to thiamine-deficient patients without thiamine is a well-documented cause of iatrogenic Wernicke encephalopathy. In any ICU/emergency patient who is malnourished, alcoholic, or has prolonged vomiting — give thiamine BEFORE or simultaneously with glucose. All TPN formulations must include thiamine.

Normal Thiamine Levels

Normal whole blood thiamine: 70–180 nmol/L. Deficiency: <70 nmol/L. ETKAC (Erythrocyte Transketolase Activation Coefficient) >1.25 indicates functional deficiency. Levels may be normal even with functional deficiency in high-demand states (sepsis, cardiac failure).

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the thiamine dose for Wernicke encephalopathy?
Wernicke encephalopathy is a medical emergency. Thiamine 500 mg IV in 100 mL NS over 30 minutes, three times daily for 2–3 days, then 250 mg IV/IM once daily for 5 days. Never give glucose before thiamine. After IV course, switch to oral thiamine 100 mg TDS indefinitely in alcoholic patients.
What is the dose of benfotiamine for diabetic neuropathy?
Benfotiamine 300 mg twice daily for 4–6 weeks (loading), then 150 mg twice daily as maintenance. Take with food — it is fat-soluble. Studies show improvement in vibration perception threshold and neuropathy scores. Available in India as Benfogamma, Milgamma N, Benfobion.
Can glucose be given before thiamine?
No. Always administer thiamine before or simultaneously with IV glucose/dextrose in thiamine-deficient or at-risk patients. IV glucose without thiamine depletes residual thiamine stores and can precipitate or dramatically worsen Wernicke encephalopathy. This applies to alcoholics, malnourished patients, those with hyperemesis, and patients on prolonged IV fluids.
What is the prophylactic thiamine dose for chronic alcoholics?
For outpatient chronic alcohol users without neurological signs: oral thiamine 100 mg/day. High-risk (heavy drinkers, poor nutrition): 100–300 mg/day orally. For inpatients undergoing alcohol detox: parenteral thiamine 100–200 mg IM/IV daily for 3–5 days, then oral maintenance. UK SIGN guidelines recommend higher parenteral doses (250–500 mg TDS) for patients at significant risk.
What is the thiamine dose in pregnancy?
RDA: 1.4 mg/day during pregnancy and lactation. Therapeutic dose for deficiency: 50–100 mg/day orally. For hyperemesis gravidarum with suspected deficiency: thiamine 100 mg IV daily until oral intake resumes. Severe deficiency in pregnancy can cause Wernicke encephalopathy — give IV thiamine before IV dextrose in vomiting pregnant patients.
What is the thiamine dose for wet beriberi?
Wet beriberi (cardiac beriberi) with haemodynamic compromise: thiamine 100 mg IV daily for 7–14 days. Cardiac improvement is often dramatic within 24–48 hours. Monitor ECG and cardiac function. Follow with oral thiamine 25–50 mg TDS for 6 weeks, then dietary correction. Treat concurrent nutritional deficiencies.
