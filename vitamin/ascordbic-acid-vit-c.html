1. Physiology & Pharmacokinetics of Ascorbic Acid

Vitamin C, chemically known as L-ascorbic acid, is a water-soluble ketolacton with powerful reducing properties. Unlike most mammals, humans lack the enzyme L-gulonolactone oxidase, making them entirely dependent on dietary intake. In the clinical setting, Vitamin C is not merely a "supplement" but a critical co-factor for at least eight enzymatic reactions.

Absorption occurs primarily in the distal small intestine via sodium-dependent vitamin C transporters (SVCT1 and SVCT2). A key pharmacokinetic principle for clinicians to remember is saturable absorption. At oral doses above 1000 mg, the percentage of absorption drops significantly (often below 50%), with the excess excreted renally. However, in states of physiological stress—such as sepsis, severe burns, or post-major surgery—metabolic demand skyrockets, often requiring parenteral (IV) administration to bypass intestinal saturation.

2. Scurvy: The Indian Clinical Picture and Treatment

While often dismissed as a historical curiosity, Scurvy (Vitamin C deficiency) remains a modern reality in India, particularly among the elderly, alcoholics, and pediatric populations with restricted diets (e.g., "tea and toast" diets or specific autism-related food aversions).

Clinical Presentation

The hallmark of scurvy is the breakdown of connective tissue. Early signs include follicular hyperkeratosis and "corkscrew hairs." As the deficiency progresses, patients present with perifollicular hemorrhage and "woody edema" of the lower limbs. In dental practice, scorbutic gingivitis presents as friable, "purple" gums that bleed with minimal provocation.

Standard Replacement Protocol

The World Health Organization (WHO) and Indian clinical standards recommend the following for confirmed scurvy:

  • Adults: 500 mg to 1000 mg daily in divided doses for at least 1 month.
  • Pediatrics: 100 mg to 300 mg daily for 2–4 weeks or until full symptomatic recovery.

Clinical recovery is often dramatic: spontaneous bleeding usually stops within 24 hours, and muscle/joint pain resolves within 48 to 72 hours. However, the associated iron-deficiency anemia may take weeks of co-supplementation to correct.

Clinical Pearl: The "Browning" of Ascorbic Acid

Clinicians should advise patients that Vitamin C is highly sensitive to light and heat. In India's warm climate, tablets or syrups that have turned brownish-orange have undergone oxidation to dehydroascorbic acid and further degradation products, significantly reducing their clinical potency.

3. Vitamin C in Wound Healing and Collagen Synthesis

Ascorbic acid is the essential co-factor for the enzymes prolyl hydroxylase and lysyl hydroxylase. These enzymes are responsible for the hydroxylation of proline and lysine residues in procollagen. Without Vitamin C, collagen fibers cannot cross-link, leading to poor tensile strength in healing tissues.

Surgical and Burn Recovery

In surgical wards across India, high-dose Vitamin C is often used as an adjunct to speed up recovery from major abdominal surgeries or large-area burns. For burn patients, the requirement can be 10 to 20 times the RDA.

Typical Protocol: 1000 mg to 2000 mg daily until the wound has achieved secondary intention closure or skin grafting has successfully "taken."

4. Synergistic Role with Iron Therapy

Anemia remains one of the most significant health burdens in India. Vitamin C plays a dual role in managing iron deficiency. First, it reduces ferric iron (Fe3+) to the more soluble ferrous (Fe2+) state. Second, it forms a chelate with iron in the acidic environment of the stomach, preventing the formation of insoluble phytate or phosphate complexes which are common in Indian vegetarian diets high in cereals and pulses.

Clinical Recommendation: For every 60mg of elemental iron, co-administration of 200mg to 500mg of Vitamin C is recommended to maximize absorption. Patients should be advised to take their iron supplement with a glass of lemon water or a Vitamin C tablet rather than tea or milk, which inhibit absorption.

5. High-Dose IV Vitamin C: Sepsis and Oncology

The use of Intravenous Vitamin C (IVC) has gained traction in Indian ICUs, particularly following the CITRIS-ALI and Marik Protocol discussions. While the "Marik Cocktail" (Vitamin C, Thiamine, and Hydrocortisone) is debated, the use of IVC to combat oxidative stress in sepsis is common.

Sepsis Dose: 1.5g to 3g IV every 6 hours for 4 days.
Oncology Context: In palliative care, extremely high-dose IVC (up to 50g-100g) is sometimes used to improve the quality of life and reduce the side effects of chemotherapy, though this must be done with strict monitoring of G6PD levels.

6. Toxicity, Contraindications, and Renal Risks

Despite being water-soluble, Vitamin C is not without risks.

  • Renal Oxalosis: Ascorbate is metabolized to oxalate. In patients with a history of calcium-oxalate kidney stones or chronic kidney disease (CKD), doses exceeding 1000mg daily can significantly increase the risk of stone formation.
  • G6PD Deficiency: High-dose IV Vitamin C can trigger hemolysis in patients with Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase deficiency. Screening is mandatory before IV protocols exceeding 15g.
  • Lab Interference: High levels of Vitamin C in the blood/urine can cause false-negative results in urine glucose (dipstick) and occult blood tests.

Conclusion

Vitamin C is a powerful clinical tool when dosed correctly. For the Indian practitioner, balancing the need for rapid replacement in deficiency states like scurvy with the risk of renal stones in a stone-prone population is key.
