How Is Fetal Growth Assessed?

Fetal growth is assessed primarily through obstetric ultrasound. The key measurements taken are:

  • BPD (Biparietal Diameter): Width of the skull at the widest point. Most accurate for gestational dating before 20 weeks.
  • HC (Head Circumference): Circumference of the fetal head. Reflects brain growth.
  • AC (Abdominal Circumference): Circumference of the fetal abdomen at the level of the liver. The most sensitive indicator of fetal nutrition and growth restriction.
  • FL (Femur Length): Length of the thigh bone. Reflects skeletal growth and long bone development.
  • CRL (Crown-Rump Length): Used only in the first trimester (up to 14 weeks) to establish gestational age.

The Hadlock EFW Formula

The Hadlock four-parameter formula is the most widely used globally for estimating fetal weight from ultrasound biometry:

log₁₀(EFW) = 1.3596 + 0.0064(HC) + 0.0424(AC) + 0.174(FL) + 0.00061(BPD × AC) − 0.00386(AC × FL)

The result is in grams. The Hadlock formula has a systematic error (standard deviation) of approximately ±7.5–15% of actual birth weight — meaning an EFW of 2,500 g could reflect a real weight of 2,125–2,875 g. This inherent imprecision is why growth trends over serial scans matter more than any single measurement.

SGA, AGA, and LGA Explained

Growth is classified by comparing EFW or birth weight to a reference population at the same gestational age:

  • SGA (Small-for-gestational-age): EFW <10th percentile. Severe SGA is <3rd percentile and may indicate fetal growth restriction (FGR).
  • AGA (Appropriate-for-gestational-age): EFW 10th–90th percentile. Normal range.
  • LGA (Large-for-gestational-age): EFW >90th percentile. Associated with gestational diabetes, macrosomia, and birth complications.

It is important to note that some SGA babies are constitutionally small (appropriate for their genetic potential) and are not at risk, while others have pathological growth restriction requiring monitoring. Your obstetrician interprets these findings alongside Doppler studies, amniotic fluid index (AFI), and clinical history.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is estimated fetal weight on ultrasound?+
Ultrasound EFW has a margin of error of approximately ±15% of actual birth weight. This means if your baby's EFW is reported as 3,000 g, the actual weight could range from approximately 2,550 to 3,450 g. Accuracy tends to be better in the second trimester and worse in the third trimester. Serial measurements tracking growth velocity are more informative than a single isolated EFW.
What does it mean if my baby is in the 30th percentile?+
A baby at the 30th percentile is within the normal range (10th–90th percentile) and is growing appropriately for gestational age. Percentile alone is less important than consistency — a baby who has been tracking at the 30th percentile throughout pregnancy is growing normally. Concern arises when there is a significant crossing of percentile lines (e.g., dropping from 50th to 10th), suggesting a deceleration in growth.
What is the average baby weight at birth?+
The average birth weight for a full-term singleton (40 weeks) is approximately 3,300–3,500 g (7 lb 4 oz – 7 lb 11 oz). Normal birth weight ranges from 2,500 g (5 lb 8 oz) to 4,000 g (8 lb 13 oz). Babies weighing less than 2,500 g at birth are classified as low birth weight (LBW), regardless of gestational age.
Does fetal sex affect growth percentiles?+
Yes. Male fetuses are on average slightly heavier than female fetuses from mid-pregnancy onwards — the difference is approximately 100–150 g at term. Sex-specific growth charts offer marginally more accurate percentile classification, particularly near the boundaries of SGA and LGA. However, combined (sex-neutral) charts are also widely used and clinically acceptable.
