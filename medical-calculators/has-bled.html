HAS-BLED — Complete Bleeding Risk Guide

   

The HAS-BLED score was developed by Pisters et al. (2010) from the Euro Heart Survey on Atrial Fibrillation, a prospective registry of 3,978 AF patients across 35 European countries. It was the first bleeding risk score specifically validated in AF patients receiving antithrombotic therapy, and it remains the most widely used and recommended bleeding risk assessment tool in AF management, endorsed by the ESC 2020 AF guidelines. The score uses nine clinical variables to estimate the annual risk of clinically relevant major bleeding — defined as requiring hospitalisation, causing haemoglobin drop of ≥2 g/dL, or requiring blood transfusion.

   

A critical — and frequently misunderstood — clinical principle: the HAS-BLED score exists to identify modifiable bleeding risk factors that should be corrected, not to justify withholding anticoagulation from patients with high stroke risk. Numerous studies have confirmed that in AF patients with CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2 (male) or ≥3 (female), the absolute stroke risk reduction from anticoagulation far exceeds the absolute bleeding risk increase — even in patients with HAS-BLED 3 or higher. The ESC 2020 guidelines are explicit: a high HAS-BLED score alone should not lead to withholding anticoagulation.

Modifiable vs Non-Modifiable HAS-BLED Factors

   

The key clinical utility of HAS-BLED is distinguishing which risk factors can be corrected to reduce bleeding risk without stopping anticoagulation:

   
         
  • Modifiable — Uncontrolled hypertension (H): Target BP <130/80 mmHg. Hypertension is the most important modifiable bleeding risk factor. Good BP control reduces both stroke and bleeding risk simultaneously
    •      
  • Modifiable — Labile INR (L): Warfarin with time in therapeutic range (TTR) <60% = elevated risk. Switch to DOAC if TTR persistently low — DOACs have more predictable anticoagulation without monitoring requirement
    •      
  • Modifiable — Drugs (D) — NSAIDs, antiplatelets: Stop NSAIDs if possible. Review antiplatelet therapy need — dual antiplatelet + anticoagulant (triple therapy) massively increases bleeding risk. Use proton pump inhibitor (PPI) if antiplatelet therapy unavoidable
    •      
  • Modifiable — Alcohol excess (A): Heavy alcohol use (>8 units/week) impairs liver function, coagulation, and adherence. Counselling and cessation support
    •      
  • Partially modifiable — Bleeding history: Prior GI bleed — treat underlying cause (H. pylori, ulcer disease, endoscopy). Prior intracranial bleed — specialist neurology/cardiology review for anticoagulation decision
    •      
  • Non-modifiable — Age >65 (E — Elderly): Cannot be modified but falls prevention, medication review, and dose adjustment for DOACs in elderly may reduce risk
    •      
  • Non-modifiable — Renal/hepatic impairment (A): Treat underlying disease. DOAC dose adjustment for CrCl <50 mL/min
    •      
  • Non-modifiable — Stroke history (S): Prior ischaemic stroke is paradoxically also a risk factor for AF-related stroke — this highlights the importance of not withholding anticoagulation based on HAS-BLED alone in stroke patients with AF
    •    
   

DOACs Reduce Bleeding Risk Compared to Warfarin

   

An important consideration in patients with elevated HAS-BLED scores: DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) are associated with significantly lower rates of intracranial haemorrhage — the most feared and often fatal type of bleeding — compared to warfarin (relative risk reduction approximately 50%). For patients with elevated HAS-BLED due to labile INR (warfarin-specific factor), switching to a DOAC both improves anticoagulation efficacy and reduces bleeding risk simultaneously. Apixaban has the most favourable bleeding profile across trials, including in elderly patients and those with CKD.

   

HAS-BLED in Patients Refusing Anticoagulation

   

When a patient refuses anticoagulation despite counselling, or when a genuine clinical decision is made to withhold anticoagulation, the HAS-BLED score can inform the rationale. However, the clinical decision should still be documented as part of shared decision-making, with clear notation that the patient understands the stroke risk. Aspirin is NOT recommended as an alternative to anticoagulation in AF — it has almost equivalent bleeding risk to DOACs with significantly inferior stroke prevention.
