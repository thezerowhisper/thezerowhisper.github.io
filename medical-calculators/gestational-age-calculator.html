What Is Gestational Age?

Gestational age (GA) is the standard clinical measure of how far along a pregnancy has progressed. It is expressed in weeks and days — for example, "10 weeks and 3 days" or commonly written as "10+3." A full-term pregnancy spans 40 weeks from the start of the last menstrual period.

Importantly, gestational age is not counted from the moment of conception. Because ovulation and fertilisation typically occur about two weeks after the last period begins, gestational age is approximately two weeks longer than the embryonic (fetal) age. This convention is used worldwide by obstetricians, midwives, and ultrasound technicians to keep a single consistent standard.

Accurate gestational dating is critical because it determines when key prenatal tests are scheduled, guides growth monitoring, informs decisions about induction of labour, and classifies deliveries as preterm (<37 weeks), term (37–42 weeks), or post-term (>42 weeks).

How Is Gestational Age Calculated?

There are three widely accepted methods for calculating gestational age. Each has specific indications and accuracy ranges:

Method Formula Accuracy Best Used When
Last Menstrual Period (LMP) GA = (Today − LMP) ÷ 7
EDD = LMP + 280 days		 ±2 weeks Regular 28-day cycles, LMP known
Ultrasound — Crown-Rump Length (CRL) CRL 1–84 mm → GA tables ±5–7 days (1st trimester) 6–13 weeks; irregular cycles
Ultrasound — Biometry (BPD, FL, HC) Hadlock nomograms ±2–3 weeks (late 2nd/3rd trimester) After 14 weeks if CRL missed
Estimated Due Date (EDD) GA = 40 weeks − Days to EDD ÷ 7 Depends on EDD source When EDD already established

Naegele's Rule — The LMP Formula Explained

The most widely used method is Naegele's Rule, developed in 1812 and still the global obstetric standard. It adds 280 days (40 weeks) to the first day of the LMP to estimate the due date:

EDD = LMP + 280 days — or equivalently — EDD = LMP + 9 months + 7 days

The rule assumes a 28-day menstrual cycle with ovulation on day 14. For cycles shorter or longer than 28 days, the EDD is adjusted by the difference: a 32-day cycle shifts the EDD four days later, while a 24-day cycle shifts it four days earlier. Our calculator applies this correction automatically.

When Ultrasound Overrides LMP Dating

ACOG (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists) recommends that ultrasound dating in the first trimester should replace LMP dating if the difference is more than 7 days (CRL <9 weeks) or more than 10 days (CRL 9.0–13.9 weeks). First-trimester ultrasound remains the gold standard when LMP is unknown, uncertain, or when cycle length is irregular.

Pregnancy Trimesters by Week

Pregnancy is divided into three trimesters. Each marks a distinct phase of fetal development and dictates which prenatal screenings, scans, and tests are appropriate:

First Trimester — Weeks 1–13

Most critical period of organ formation.

  • Week 6: Fetal heartbeat detectable
  • Week 8: All major organs forming
  • Week 10: Nuchal translucency scan
  • Week 11–13: NIPT / First-trimester screening
Second Trimester — Weeks 14–27

Growth and maturation phase.

  • Week 16: Quadruple marker screen
  • Week 18–22: Anatomy ultrasound
  • Week 20: Fetal movements felt
  • Week 24: Viability threshold
Third Trimester — Weeks 28–40+

Rapid weight gain and preparation for birth.

  • Week 28: GDM screen & Rh immunoglobulin
  • Week 32–34: Growth scan
  • Week 36: GBS screening
  • Week 40: Full-term delivery

Preterm, Term, and Post-Term Definitions

The World Health Organization and ACOG classify gestational age at delivery as follows:

  • Extremely preterm: <28 weeks
  • Very preterm: 28 to <32 weeks
  • Moderate to late preterm: 32 to <37 weeks
  • Early term: 37+0 to 38+6 weeks
  • Full term: 39+0 to 40+6 weeks
  • Late term: 41+0 to 41+6 weeks
  • Post-term: 42+0 weeks and beyond

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between gestational age and fetal age? +
Gestational age is counted from the first day of your last menstrual period (LMP), while fetal age (also called embryonic age or conceptional age) is counted from the date of fertilisation. Because ovulation typically occurs about 14 days into a 28-day cycle, fetal age is approximately 2 weeks less than gestational age. Clinicians always use gestational age because the exact date of conception is rarely known, whereas the LMP date is reliable.
How accurate is gestational age calculated by LMP? +
LMP dating is accurate to within approximately ±2 weeks. It assumes a 28-day cycle with ovulation on day 14. The accuracy decreases for women with irregular cycles, cycles shorter than 21 or longer than 35 days, recent hormonal contraceptive use, or breastfeeding. In these cases, first-trimester ultrasound provides a significantly more accurate gestational age.
Can gestational age change after an ultrasound? +
Yes. If the ultrasound dating differs from the LMP date by more than 7 days in the first trimester (before 9 weeks) or more than 10 days at 9–13 weeks, clinical guidelines (ACOG, NICE) recommend updating the estimated due date to the ultrasound-based date. After 20 weeks, the EDD should generally not be changed even if growth differs, as later ultrasounds are less accurate for dating and differences may reflect fetal growth variation rather than incorrect dating.
How is gestational age measured in weeks vs. months? +
Healthcare providers always use weeks, not calendar months, to describe gestational age — because "month" is ambiguous (calendar months vary in length) and because pregnancy lasts 10 lunar months but only about 9 calendar months. If you want an approximate conversion: divide the gestational week by 4.33 (average weeks per calendar month). So 28 weeks ≈ 6.5 calendar months.
What week is the anatomy scan done? +
The fetal anatomy scan (also called the anomaly scan or mid-pregnancy scan) is typically performed between 18 and 22 weeks of gestation. At this stage, all major fetal organs are sufficiently developed to be assessed. It checks brain, heart, spine, kidneys, limbs, placenta location, and amniotic fluid levels. Accurate gestational dating before this scan ensures the measurements are interpreted using the correct reference ranges.
Is gestational age the same as weeks pregnant? +
Yes — "weeks pregnant" and "gestational age" mean the same thing in everyday and clinical language. When your doctor says you are "12 weeks pregnant," they mean your gestational age is 12 weeks (counting from the first day of your last period). The two terms are used interchangeably.
What is corrected gestational age for premature babies? +
Corrected age (also called adjusted age) is used for preterm infants after birth. It accounts for the weeks the baby was born early: Corrected age = Chronological age − Weeks born early. For example, a 6-month-old baby born 10 weeks early has a corrected age of approximately 3.7 months. Paediatricians use this to set appropriate developmental milestones up to age 2–3 years.
